Epinephrine

2 / 9

Also called Adrenalin and EpiPen.

It's a sympathomimetic, catecholamine, and vasopressor — it mimics your body's own stress response.

First-line for both anaphylaxis and cardiac arrest.

The reason it works for both comes down to which receptors it hits…

ROB THE PARAMEDIC

Mechanism

3 / 9

It hits three receptors at once, and each one does something different.

α-1 causes vasoconstriction, which brings BP back up.

β-1 increases HR and contractility.

β-2 relaxes the airways.

Which receptor matters most depends on why you're giving it…

Dose

4 / 9

Cardiac Arrest

1 mg IV/IO (1:10,000) every 3–5 min.

Push fast and flush with 20 mL NS after each dose to move it centrally.

Anaphylaxis is a different situation entirely…

ROB THE PARAMEDIC

Dose

5 / 9

Anaphylaxis

0.3–0.5 mg IM (1:1,000) into the anterolateral thigh.

That's ten times more concentrated than the arrest dose — the concentration matters as much as the dose.

Repeat every 5–15 min if there's no improvement.

Before you give it — know the contraindications…

ROB THE PARAMEDIC

Cautions

6 / 9

Epi has relative contraindications — not hard stops, but situations where the cardiovascular load becomes a real concern.

Coronary insufficiency
Epi increases myocardial O₂ demand, which can worsen ischemia.
Uncontrolled HTN
Carries the same concern.

There are no absolute contraindications in cardiac arrest.

One more thing — and it trips people up every time…

ROB THE PARAMEDIC

Clinical Pearl

7 / 9

The concentration mix-up is one of the most common errors with this drug.

1:10,000 is for IV push in arrest.

1:1,000 is for IM in anaphylaxis.

Getting them reversed means a tenfold dosing error.
