Identity

Midazolam is a benzodiazepine — also called Versed.

It enhances GABA at the GABA-A receptor. More chloride into the neuron, the neuron quiets down, and the uncontrolled firing that drives a seizure stops.

Used for seizures, agitation, and procedural sedation — but seizures are where it earns its reputation in the field.

Why Timing Matters

A seizure beyond 5 minutes is status epilepticus. The longer it runs, the harder it is to stop — and the more neuronal damage accumulates.

Get midazolam on board fast. Not after access. Not after a full assessment. As soon as you confirm the patient is seizing.

Dosing

4 / 8

IM

0.2 mg/kg IM, max 10 mg. Onset 5–10 min.

Anterolateral thigh, done. No IV needed, no delay — fastest way to get the drug working on arrival.

There are two other routes worth knowing…

Dosing

5 / 8

IN and IV

IN: 0.2 mg/kg, max 10 mg, onset 3–5 min. Split between both nares — full volume in one nostril causes runoff. Use the 5 mg/mL concentration.

IV: 0.1 mg/kg, max 4 mg, onset 1–3 min. Fastest onset, but requires access you may not have on a seizing patient.

Precautions

6 / 8

After the Seizure Stops

A postictal patient already has a depressed respiratory drive. Versed could make this worse.

Respiratory depression and apnea are the primary risks. Alcohol or opioids on board make it worse.

Monitor RR and SpO2 continuously. Have BVM and suction ready. Elderly patients — cut the dose in half.
