# Shift-Story Carousel Guide

Everything needed to write a shift-story carousel: how it sounds, how it's structured, and a worked example. Load this file in full before writing any slides.

---

## Voice

Write like you're telling a friend about a call on the drive back to the station. Not writing — *talking*. The difference matters.

- **Let thoughts breathe.** A slide can have line breaks between beats without being fragmented. Space creates rhythm.
- **Don't over-fragment.** Some moments belong together on one slide. "I took him and started compressions on the kitchen table. My partner bagged." — that's one slide, not two.
- **Soften transitions naturally.** "And we got ROSC in about four minutes." The "And" is intentional — it's how people actually talk.
- **Let the reflection do real work.** Don't rush to the lesson. The narrator sitting with the moment IS part of the story.
- **Concrete lessons, not vague sentiment.** "It's best to assume that screen with call notes is just wrong" beats "always be prepared." Say the actual thing.
- No color tags. No section labels. No clinical lecturer voice.
- One emoji max per carousel, hook only, only if it fits naturally.

---

## Planning

Before writing, answer these two questions:

1. **What's the emotional arc?** Where does the narrator start emotionally, and where do they end up?
2. **What's the concrete lesson?** One specific, actionable truth this call revealed — not a vague sentiment. If you can't state it plainly, keep thinking.

---

## Story Arc

Every shift-story moves through these beats. Slide count is flexible — use as many as the story needs, no padding.

1. **Hook** — Drop into the moment. Multiple short lines with breathing room. End with tension or a cliffhanger.
2. **Dispatch** — The call as it came in. One or two slides, sparse.
3. **Expectation** — What you thought you were walking into.
4. **Reality** — What you actually found. This is the turn.
5. **Action** — What you did. One clean slide. Don't over-fragment.
6. **Outcome** — What happened. Keep it honest. Emotion is fine here.
7. **Reflection** — Let the narrator sit with it. This slide does real work — don't rush through it to get to the lesson.
8. **Lesson** — Start with `The lesson:` on its own line, then the takeaway. Concrete and specific, not motivational.
9. **CTA** — Two lines. Simple.

---

## Hook Rules

Multiple short lines, each landing on its own. Build tension beat by beat. End with "…" or an open question that makes the reader swipe.

Don't introduce yourself. Don't set the scene. Start inside the moment.

**Example:**
```
[1] Had my first CPR as a medic yesterday.

    It was an infant.

    And I learned something I'll never forget…
```

---

## Lesson Slide

Always explicit. Lead with `The lesson:` on its own line, then state it directly.

**Wrong:** "Always be ready for anything."
**Right:**
```
The lesson:

The call you show up to is almost never the call you were dispatched to.
In fact it's best to assume that screen with call notes is just wrong.
People depend on our preparation.
```

---

## Slide Limits

- **200 characters max per slide** (markup stripped). Sparse is the point — each slide is one moment.
- The reflection slide can push closer to the limit. It's earned.

---

## Slide Format

No section labels. No teasers. Line breaks within a slide are fine — use them to create rhythm.

```
[1] Hook line.

    Second beat.

    Third beat…

[2] Dispatch. One or two lines.

[3] What you expected.

[4] What you found.
    One more line if needed.

[5] What you did. This can be a full sentence or two — don't split it up.

[6] Outcome.
    Emotional beat.

[7] Reflection. Let it breathe. This is the narrator sitting with the moment.

[8] The lesson:

    The concrete takeaway.

[9] CTA line 1
    CTA line 2
```

---

## CTA Format

Two lines. Simple and human — match the tone of the story.

**Line 1 — Engagement (rotate one per carousel):**
```
If you've been there, you know.
Have you had a call like this? Tell me in the comments.
What's a call that stuck with you? Drop it in the comments.
What would you have done differently? Let me know in the comments.
```

**Line 2 — Channel hint:**
```
Follow for more medic wisdom.
```

Keep it simple. No clinical quiz format. No sales language.

## Worked Example: Infant CPR

**Opener:** "Had my first CPR as a medic yesterday. It was an infant."
**Lesson:** "The call you show up to is almost never the call you were dispatched to."

```
[1] Had my first CPR as a medic yesterday.

    It was an infant.

    And I learned something I'll never forget…

[2] Tones dropped at 3am.
    "Infant not breathing normally."

[3] I was expecting a sick baby.
    Maybe a febrile seizure.

[4] Mom met us at the door with the baby.

    He was blue and limp in her arms.

[5] This was a full arrest.

    Not what I prepared for on the drive over.

[6] I took him and started compressions on the kitchen table. My partner bagged.

[7] And we got ROSC in about four minutes.

    He started crying. Mom started crying.

[8] After we dropped Mom and baby off at the ER, I sat in the ambulance for
    20 minutes, just thinking about how unprepared I was for that situation.

[9] The lesson:

    The call you show up to is almost never the call you were dispatched to.
    In fact it's best to assume that screen with call notes is just wrong.
    People depend on our preparation.

[10] If you've been there, you know.
     Follow for more medic wisdom.
```

**Why this works:**
- Hook breaks into three beats with breathing room — each line lands on its own before the next hits.
- Expectation slide sets up the turn. The gap between what was expected and what was found IS the story.
- Action is one clean slide, not split up. The moment doesn't need commentary.
- Outcome lets emotion land naturally — no editorializing needed.
- Reflection slide does real work. The narrator sitting in the ambulance for 20 minutes is more honest than any summarizing sentence.
- Lesson is concrete and specific, not motivational. "Assume that screen is just wrong" is something a medic can actually carry.
- CTA matches the tone — personal, not clinical.
