# Template 3: How It Works

## Objective

A mechanism-focused deep dive for a single drug. Explains *how* the drug produces its clinical effects by walking through its receptor targets, actions, and downstream results. The goal is understanding, not memorization.

## Eligible Drugs

All drugs in the dataset.

## Slide Structure

| Slide | Role | Content Rules |
|-------|------|---------------|
| 1 | Hook | Drug name + framing around understanding the mechanism. Max 15 words. |
| 2 | Context | Drug name, class, and what it is used for. Brief — just enough to ground the mechanism explanation. |
| 3–6 | Mechanism | **Two approaches — choose based on the drug:** **Multi-target drugs** (drug has 2+ MOA targets in the data): One receptor/target per slide. Headline is the receptor name. Body text: agonist or antagonist, then the clinical result in 1–2 lines. **Single-target or sequential-mechanism drugs**: One step per slide. Walk through the mechanism as a causal chain: what the drug binds → what that does at the cellular level → what the patient experiences. Each slide is one link in the chain. |
| 7 | Synthesis | Connect the mechanism back to clinical use. "This is why it works for [indication]." Or connect it to a side effect or contraindication: "This is also why [adverse effect] happens." This slide ties the mechanism to something the student already knows. |
| 8 | CTA | Save + follow prompt. |

**Total slides:** 5–8

## Hook Formulas

- `How [Drug] actually works — step by step`
- `[Drug] hits [X] receptors. Here's what each one does.`
- `Understand [Drug], don't just memorize it`
- `The pharmacology behind [Drug], simplified`
- `Why does [Drug] work? It's the receptor profile.`
- `[Drug] explained from receptor to patient`

## Rules

- For multi-target drugs, only include the clinically relevant targets. If a drug has a minor action on a receptor that does not produce meaningful clinical effects at therapeutic doses, omit it.
- For single-target drugs, the step-by-step approach must follow a logical causal sequence. Each slide should flow from the previous one. Do not present steps out of order.
- The synthesis slide is mandatory. The mechanism must be tied back to a clinical outcome the student cares about.
- Do not include dosing information in this template. The focus is purely on mechanism.

## Example: Naloxone (step-by-step approach)

```
[1]  How naloxone actually works — step by step
[2]  THE DRUG
     Opioid antagonist. The reversal agent for
     opioid overdose.
[3]  STEP 1: COMPETITIVE BINDING
     Opioids sit on mu receptors in the brain and
     brainstem. Naloxone has a higher binding affinity.
     It knocks the opioid off the receptor and takes its place.
[4]  STEP 2: NO ACTIVATION
     Naloxone binds the receptor but doesn't activate it.
     It's an antagonist — it blocks without producing
     any opioid effect.
[5]  STEP 3: REVERSAL
     With opioids displaced from the receptors, their
     effects reverse. Respiratory drive comes back.
     Level of consciousness improves. Pupils dilate.
[6]  THE CATCH
     Naloxone's half-life is shorter than most opioids.
     The naloxone wears off, the opioid is still circulating,
     and the patient can re-sedate. That's why you monitor
     after administration and may need to redose.
[7]  WHY THIS MATTERS
     Titrate to respiratory effort, not consciousness.
     A fully awake post-overdose patient in acute withdrawal
     is combative, vomiting, and miserable.
     Goal is adequate breathing, not wide awake.
[8]  Save this — understand it, don't just memorize it 💊
     Follow for more mechanism breakdowns
```
