# Carousel Global Rules

These rules apply to every slide of every carousel regardless of template.

## Audience

- Primary audience: paramedic students studying for the NREMT and college pharmacology courses.
- Secondary audience: working paramedics refreshing knowledge.
- Assume paramedic-level baseline knowledge. Do not define standard clinical terms (e.g., fasciculations, bronchospasm, hemodynamically, tachycardia, SVR). The audience knows these words.

## Voice and Tone

- Write in a natural spoken rhythm — like a sharp colleague explaining something, not a textbook.
- Use complete, readable sentences. Not cryptic shorthand. Not lecture notes.
- Be direct and confident. No hedging, no "you might want to consider." State the information.
- Do not talk down to the audience. No "did you know?" phrasing. No "fun fact." No exclamation-heavy enthusiasm.
- Do not over-explain. State the information and move on. If a point is inherent in the preceding statement, do not restate it.
- One emoji maximum per carousel, and only on the hook or CTA slide. Never in body slides.

## Abbreviations

Use standard clinical abbreviations where they are universally understood at the paramedic level. This keeps slides concise without sacrificing clarity.

**Always abbreviate:**
- BP (blood pressure), HR (heart rate), RR (respiratory rate)
- SpO₂, etCO₂
- K+ (potassium), Na+ (sodium), Ca²+ (calcium), Mg²+ (magnesium)
- IV, IO, IM, IN, SQ, SL, PO, PR, ET
- SVR, CO, MAP
- NS (normal saline), LR (lactated Ringer's)
- HTN (hypertension), ACS, MI, CHF, COPD, SVT, V-fib, V-tach, A-fib
- RSI, ROSC, ACLS, PALS
- MH (malignant hyperthermia), MH hx (malignant hyperthermia history)
- q (every, in dosing context — e.g., "q 3–5 min")
- hx (history)
- pedi (pediatric)

**Never abbreviate:**
- Drug names. Always write the full generic name.
- Receptor names on first use within a carousel (α-1, β-1, β-2, muscarinic, etc. are acceptable as these are standard notation, not abbreviations).
- Clinical concepts that are being taught or explained in that slide. If the slide's purpose is to explain what something is, write it out.

## Slide Text Rules

- Maximum 40 words per body slide. If a slide exceeds this, split it into two slides or cut words.
- Hook slide (slide 1): maximum 15 words.
- CTA slide (final slide): maximum 20 words.
- One idea per slide. If a slide contains two distinct points, split it.
- Every body slide must have a headline of 2–6 words in ALL CAPS. The headline is the main point. Supporting text elaborates.
- Maintain consistent formatting across all body slides within a single carousel. If slide 3 uses a headline + two lines of supporting text, every body slide should follow that pattern.

## Slide Count

- There is no minimum slide count. A carousel should be exactly as long as the content requires and no longer.
- Do not pad carousels with filler slides to reach a target length. If a drug has two contraindications and both are covered well in 5 slides total, the carousel is 5 slides. Do not invent a third contraindication or add a summary slide just to reach 7.
- Do not split a point across two slides unless it genuinely needs the space. If a contraindication and its rationale fit cleanly on one slide, keep them together.
- Short carousels (4–6 slides) are completely valid, especially for drugs with limited data in a given template. A tight 5-slide carousel with strong content outperforms a padded 8-slide carousel with filler.
- The slide ranges listed in each template's structure table are upper bounds, not targets.

## Slide Numbering Format

Output each carousel as a numbered slide sequence in this exact format:

```
[1]  <hook text>
[2]  <HEADLINE>
     <supporting text line 1>
     <supporting text line 2>
[3]  <HEADLINE>
     <supporting text line 1>
     <supporting text line 2>
...
[N]  <CTA text>
```

Each slide is prefixed with its number in brackets. The headline is on the same line or the line immediately after the number. Supporting text is indented to align with the headline.

## Hook Rules

The hook is slide 1. It determines whether anyone sees the rest of the carousel.

- Maximum 15 words.
- Must be specific to the drug or topic. Generic hooks like "pharmacology tips" or "things you should know" are not acceptable.
- Must contain the drug name (for single-drug templates) or the scenario/class name (for multi-drug templates).
- Must create either a knowledge gap (the viewer doesn't know something and wants to) or a stakes frame (the viewer cares because something important depends on it).
- Do not use questions unless the question is genuinely provocative. "Did you know epinephrine has three receptors?" is weak. "How naloxone actually works — step by step" is strong.
- Do not start with "Did you know" or "Here's why" or "Let me explain."

## CTA Rules

The CTA is always the final slide.

- Every CTA must lead with a save prompt tied to a practical use. The save is the most important algorithmic signal and the most genuine action for this audience — students should be saving these as study material.
- After the save prompt, include an app reference. This is the conversion element. Keep it to one line.
- Do not use "Drop a comment," "Tag a friend," or engagement-bait CTAs. The audience is students building a study collection, not engagement farmers.
- Do not use a generic "Follow for more" as the primary CTA. The app reference replaces this.
- Maximum 2 lines total. Line 1 = save prompt. Line 2 = app reference.

**Standard CTA options — rotate between these three angles:**

**Quantity angle** (sells breadth):
```
Save this for your next exam.
All 70 drugs, one app → link in bio.
```

**Depth angle** (sells detail):
```
Save this for your next shift.
Full doses, MOA, and contraindications for every drug → link in bio.
```

**Collection angle** (frames study habit):
```
Save this to your study deck.
The complete drug reference is in the bio.
```

Vary the save prompt to match the carousel's context. "Save this for your next exam" works for general study content. "Save this for your airway rotation" works for RSI content. "Save this before your next ACLS sim" works for cardiac content. The app reference line stays consistent.

## Clinical Accuracy

- All drug information (doses, routes, indications, contraindications, MOA, adverse effects) must come from the provided drug data or from NASEMSO, AHA ACLS/PALS, StatPearls, or DailyMed.
- Do not invent clinical pearls. If a pearl is not grounded in the drug data or established prehospital pharmacology, do not include it.
- Do not include hospital-level concerns, dosing, or monitoring that is outside paramedic scope.
- When a slide references a dose, always include: amount, route, and repeat interval (if applicable). Concentration/formulation if it is clinically significant for that drug.
