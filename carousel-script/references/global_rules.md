# Carousel Global Rules

These rules apply to every carousel, regardless of type.

---

## Audience

Paramedic students (NREMT, college pharm) and working medics. Assume paramedic-level baseline — don't define standard clinical terms (fasciculations, bronchospasm, tachycardia, SVR, etc.).

---

## The Core Principle: Tell a Story

A carousel is a narrative, not a flashcard set. Each slide picks up where the last left off. Information builds. By the end, the viewer understands something they didn't before.

**Before writing, identify the through-line:** the one thing the viewer walks away understanding. Every slide serves it. If a slide doesn't move forward, cut or reframe it.

---

## Carousel Structure

Every carousel follows this general shape. Depth and slide count flexes by topic and type.

1. **Hook** — Teases the angle. Makes the reader want to swipe.
2. **Context** — Grounds the reader. What is this about and why does it matter?
3. **Dive** — The core content the pitch is about.
4. **Synthesis** — Ties the dive back to something the reader cares about. Not a recap — a landing thought.
5. **CTA** — Engagement question + resource hint.

---

## Lead Line First

Every body slide opens with a **lead line** — a complete sentence that can stand alone. Most important thought first, then build.

### No Throat-Clearing

Open with what matters. No roundabout setups.

**Wrong:** "When it comes to treating bradycardia, there's one drug that stands out."
**Right:** "Atropine is the first-line drug for symptomatic bradycardia."

---

## Abbreviations

**Always:** BP, HR, RR, SpO₂, etCO₂, K+, Na+, Ca²+, Mg²+, IV, IO, IM, IN, SQ, SL, PO, PR, ET, SVR, CO, MAP, NS, LR, HTN, ACS, MI, CHF, COPD, SVT, V-fib, V-tach, A-fib, RSI, ROSC, ACLS, PALS, MH, q, hx, pedi

**Never abbreviate:** Drug names, receptor names on first use, clinical concepts being explained on that slide.
