# Template 2: When NOT to Give

## Objective

A single-drug contraindication deep dive. Every reason to hold the drug, each explored with enough depth that the student understands *why* it is contraindicated — not just that it is.

## Eligible Drugs

All drugs in the dataset.

## Slide Structure

| Slide | Role | Content Rules |
|-------|------|---------------|
| 1 | Hook | Drug name + danger/stakes framing. Max 15 words. |
| 2 | Context | What the drug is and what it does, in one or two lines. The contraindications need this context to make sense. |
| 3–7 | Contraindications | **If the drug has 3+ contraindications:** One per slide. Contraindication as headline, 1–3 lines of rationale underneath. The rationale must connect the drug's pharmacology to why this condition makes it dangerous. **If the drug has 1–2 contraindications:** Go deeper. Spend multiple slides per contraindication: one slide for what the contraindication is, one for why the pharmacology makes it dangerous, one for what happens clinically if you give it anyway. |
| Last | CTA | Save + follow prompt. |

**Total slides:** 5–9

## Hook Formulas

- `[Drug] — [X] times you need to hold it`
- `Stop. Before you push [Drug], check these.`
- `When NOT to give [Drug] (this is the part they test)`
- `[Drug] can hurt your patient. Here's when.`

## Rules

- Every contraindication slide must include a rationale. Do not just list the contraindication as a bare fact. Explain *why* — connect it to the drug's mechanism or the patient's physiology.
- If a drug has no absolute contraindications (e.g., epinephrine in cardiac arrest), state that explicitly and focus on relative contraindications and use-caution scenarios.
- Order contraindications from most dangerous / most commonly tested to least.
- If multiple contraindications share the same underlying mechanism (e.g., several conditions that all cause hyperkalemia risk with succinylcholine), it is acceptable to explain the mechanism once and reference it in subsequent slides. Do not repeat the full explanation.

## Example: Succinylcholine

```
[1]  Succinylcholine can kill your patient.
     Here's when to hold it. 🛑
[2]  WHAT IT DOES
     Depolarizing paralytic for RSI.
     Fastest onset of any neuromuscular blocker.
[3]  🛑 HYPERKALEMIA
     Succs causes a transient K+ spike every time you give it.
     If K+ is already elevated, that bump can push them
     into cardiac arrest.
[4]  🛑 BURN / CRUSH INJURIES (>48 hrs old)
     After major tissue damage the body upregulates receptors
     along the muscle. Succs hits all of them at once and you
     get a massive K+ release.
[5]  🛑 HISTORY OF MALIGNANT HYPERTHERMIA
     Personal or family history. Succs is a known trigger.
     Classic board question.
[6]  🛑 NEUROMUSCULAR DISEASE
     Muscular dystrophy, denervation injuries — same
     receptor upregulation as burns. Same K+ danger.
[7]  🛑 PENETRATING EYE INJURY
     Fasciculations raise intraocular pressure.
     That can worsen a globe rupture.
[8]  Save this. Know your contraindications cold. ❄️
     Follow for more drug breakdowns
```

## Example: Naloxone (short carousel — single contraindication, explored in depth)

```
[1]  When NOT to give naloxone 🛑
[2]  WHAT IT DOES
     Opioid antagonist. Reverses opioid overdose
     by displacing opioids from mu receptors.
[3]  🛑 HYPERSENSITIVITY
     The only absolute contraindication.
     True naloxone allergy is rare but documented.
[4]  THE REAL DANGER ISN'T A CONTRAINDICATION
     It's over-reversal. Naloxone given too fast or
     in too high a dose precipitates acute withdrawal:
     combativeness, vomiting, pulmonary edema.
[5]  WHAT THAT MEANS IN PRACTICE
     Titrate to respiratory effort, not consciousness.
     Goal is adequate breathing — not wide awake.
[6]  Save this — especially before clinical rotations 💊
     Follow for more drug breakdowns
```
