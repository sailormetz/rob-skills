# Topic Guidelines

Derived from the 13 topics in our current queue. These patterns are what make them work.

---

## The Core Pattern

Every good topic is: **a drug + a tension.** The drug is the subject. The tension is why someone would swipe. Without tension, it's a flashcard. With it, it's a story.

Look at what all 13 share:
- "Fentanyl — safe when used right, **dangerous when it's not**"
- "Epinephrine does everything — **and that's what makes it confusing**"
- "Naloxone reverses opioids, **but dosing it wrong creates a new emergency**"
- "Atropine speeds the heart up. **But there are bradycardias it can't fix.**"

The tension is the "but." Every good pitch has one — explicitly or implied.

---

## Tension Types (from our existing topics)

### "Works, but not always"
The drug does its job — except in specific situations. The carousel explains the exceptions.
- *Atropine speeds the heart up by blocking the vagus nerve. But there are bradycardias it can't fix.*
- *Amiodarone in cardiac arrest — when to give it, how it works, and why it's not the first thing you reach for.*

### "One drug, many faces"
The drug behaves differently depending on dose, route, or indication. The carousel untangles the complexity.
- *Epinephrine does everything — one drug, different doses, different concentrations, different routes.*
- *Dopamine changes personality with the dose. Low, medium, high — three different drugs in one drip.*

### "The common mistake"
There's a standard practice that's wrong or oversimplified. The carousel makes the case for the better approach.
- *Naloxone reverses opioids, but dosing it wrong creates a new emergency. The case for titrating instead of slamming 2 mg.*

### "When NOT to give it"
The contraindications or danger zones are the story. The carousel walks through each one with clinical reasoning.
- *The reasons you don't give fentanyl and how to spot them.*
- *Nitro drops preload and afterload, but the contraindications are what trip people up on the test.*
- *The fastest paralytic in EMS — how it works, why it wears off so quick, and the patients you can't give it to.*

### "What actually happens"
The pitch promises the viewer will understand the real mechanism or clinical experience — not just the textbook summary.
- *What actually happens when you push adenosine. The six-second pause, why it works, and when it doesn't.*
- *Albuterol for bronchospasm — what beta-2 activation actually does to the airway and when to escalate.*

### "Head-to-head"
Two drugs, same job or same class. The carousel helps the viewer decide when to pick which.
- *Morphine vs fentanyl — same drug class, different situations. When you'd pick one over the other.*
- *Ketamine for pain in the field — why some protocols are choosing it over fentanyl and what makes it different.*

### "Single indication, full story"
One drug, one clinical scenario, told completely — mechanism, dosing, what to watch for.
- *Midazolam for seizures — how it stops a seizure, the dosing by route, and what to watch for after.*

## Pitch Format

1-2 sentences. Must name the drug. Must create a knowledge gap — something the viewer realizes they're not sure about.

**What the best pitches do:**
- Promise specificity (numbers, scenarios, routes — not "everything about")
- Sound like a preceptor introducing a teaching moment, not a textbook chapter title
- Imply there's something the viewer thinks they know but actually doesn't

**Wrong:**
- "Everything you need to know about naloxone." (no angle, no tension)
- "Naloxone pharmacology overview." (textbook title)
- "Naloxone is important for opioid reversal." (states a fact, doesn't create a gap)

**Right:**
- "Naloxone reverses opioids, but dosing it wrong creates a new emergency."

---

## Rules

- One drug per topic. Comparisons name both but pick one as the `drug` field.
- Don't duplicate angles. "Fentanyl contraindications" and "when not to give fentanyl" = same topic.
- Mix tension types across batches. Don't generate 5 "when NOT to give it" topics in a row.
- Prioritize drugs students find confusing or that have interesting clinical nuance over obscure drugs.
- The pitch must sound conversational. If it reads like a chapter heading, rewrite it.
